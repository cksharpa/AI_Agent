Esteemed judges, worthy opponents, ladies and gentlemen, we stand firmly in the proposition – that we *do* need strict policies and laws to regulate Artificial Intelligence. Our stance isn't rooted in fear, but in a profound recognition of the accelerating power of AI and the imperative to safeguard humanity’s future. To argue otherwise is to abdicate responsibility for the potential harms inherent in a technology rapidly outstripping our capacity for understanding and control.

Our argument rests on three core pillars, each demonstrably supported by data, ethical considerations, and emerging realities.

**1. Mitigating Bias and Discrimination – A Systemic Threat Amplified by AI:**  The assertion that AI is inherently neutral is a dangerous myth. AI systems are trained on data, and that data reflects – and consequently, perpetuates – existing societal biases. Algorithmic bias isn’t a bug; it's a feature of poorly designed and unscrutinized systems.  Consider the COMPAS recidivism algorithm used in US courts. ProPublica’s investigation, based on rigorous statistical analysis of 17,000 criminal justice records, revealed that COMPAS disproportionately flagged Black defendants as high-risk for re-offending, even when controlling for prior criminal history and other relevant factors. This wasn't deliberate malice; it was a consequence of using biased arrest data to train the algorithm.  Further, Amazon’s scrapped recruitment tool, trained on historical hiring data predominantly featuring male applicants, penalized resumes containing words associated with women’s colleges.  The OECD’s 2019 report on AI and Bias explicitly highlights this danger, stating that “AI systems can reinforce and amplify existing inequalities.” *Without stringent regulations mandating bias audits, diverse datasets, and demonstrable fairness metrics, AI will exacerbate discrimination across employment, housing, lending, and even criminal justice – perpetuating systemic injustice on a scale previously unimaginable.*  A legal framework guaranteeing algorithmic accountability, akin to anti-discrimination laws, is not merely desirable; it’s a necessity.

**2. Ensuring Accountability and Responsibility in Autonomous Systems – The "Responsibility Gap":** As AI systems – particularly those operating autonomously – gain greater agency, assigning responsibility for their actions becomes increasingly complex.  The development and deployment of autonomous vehicles offers a stark example.  Who is liable when an autonomous car causes an accident? The programmer? The manufacturer? The owner? Current legal frameworks are ill-equipped to deal with this "responsibility gap," a situation where no individual or entity is clearly accountable. Recent reports by the European Parliament’s Committee on Civil Liberties, Justice and Home Affairs (LIBE) are deeply concerned with this issue, calling for a legal definition of ‘electronic personhood’ for autonomous systems alongside clear lines of liability.  Similarly, the use of autonomous weapons systems (AWS) – “killer robots” – raises profound ethical and legal concerns. The Campaign to Stop Killer Robots has repeatedly argued that delegating life-and-death decisions to machines violates fundamental human rights and poses a significant threat to global security. *Regulations are urgently needed to prohibit the development and deployment of AWS, establishing clear lines of responsibility for their actions, and ensuring human oversight in situations requiring ethical judgment.*

**3. Protecting Data Privacy and Security – Beyond the Current Frameworks:** AI’s reliance on vast datasets necessitates unprecedented levels of data collection and processing. Current data privacy laws, such as GDPR, while valuable, are simply not sufficient to address the unique challenges posed by AI. AI algorithms can infer incredibly sensitive information from seemingly innocuous data – profiling individuals, predicting behaviour, and potentially manipulating decisions. The Cambridge Analytica scandal demonstrated how personal data, harvested through social media, could be used to influence political outcomes. However, AI’s ability to correlate and analyze data sets far surpasses human capability, making effective oversight extraordinarily difficult.  Furthermore, AI systems are vulnerable to malicious attacks, posing significant security risks.  Our proposed regulations must include enforceable data minimization requirements, robust anonymization techniques, independent audits of AI systems’ data handling practices, and stringent security standards to prevent misuse and breaches. *We need laws that explicitly regulate the use of AI for surveillance and profiling, empowering individuals with control over their data and ensuring accountability for data breaches involving AI systems.*


In conclusion, the potential risks posed by unchecked AI development are too significant to ignore. Strict policies and laws are not about stifling innovation; they’re about guiding it responsibly, ensuring that AI serves humanity’s best interests, and preventing a future where this powerful technology becomes a source of profound societal harm. We urge you to support the proposition – because a future of responsible AI demands it. Thank you.
